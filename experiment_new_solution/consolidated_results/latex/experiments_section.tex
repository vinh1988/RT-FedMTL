\section{EXPERIMENTS}
\label{sec:experiments}

\subsection{Experimental Setup}

\subsubsection{Datasets and Tasks}
The evaluation employs three core NLU benchmarks representing diverse natural language understanding challenges from the GLUE benchmark \cite{wang2018glue}.

SST-2 \cite{socher2013sst} is a binary classification task for sentiment polarity detection, comprising 66,477 training, 872 validation, and 1,821 test samples. Performance is measured by accuracy and F1-score \cite{vanrijsbergen1979}.

QQP \cite{sharma2019qqp} is a binary classification task for paraphrase detection, with 323,415 training, 40,431 validation, and 39,081 test samples. Evaluation uses accuracy and F1-score \cite{vanrijsbergen1979}.

STS-B \cite{cer2017stsb} is a regression task for semantic similarity scoring over sentence pairs on a 1–5 scale, with 4,249 training, 1,500 validation, and 1,379 test samples. Performance is measured by Pearson \cite{pearson1895} and Spearman correlation \cite{spearman1904}.

\subsubsection{Federated Data Partitioning}

IID Partitioning distributes data uniformly across clients (Experiments 5--6 in Table~\ref{tab:design_matrix}, 3 clients, multi-task scenarios).

Non-IID Partitioning uses a Dirichlet distribution ($\alpha{=}0.5$) to simulate realistic data heterogeneity, with 3 clients per task (9 total), yielding 107,805 / 22,159 / 1,416 samples per client for QQP, SST-2, and STS-B respectively (Experiments 7--10 in Table~\ref{tab:design_matrix}).

Validation sets are \emph{not} split across clients in any paradigm, ensuring consistent global model assessment.

\subsubsection{Baselines}
Three baselines are evaluated: (1) Centralized MTL: all 5 SLM variants trained on centralized data with shared encoder and task-specific heads, serving as the upper-bound reference (Exp.\ 1 in Table~\ref{tab:design_matrix}); (2) Federated Single-Task: per-task federated experiments with 3 clients and non-IID data (Exps.\ 8--10 in Table~\ref{tab:design_matrix}); and (3) Large-model federated approaches: comparison against larger LMs to assess communication efficiency and performance trade-offs.

\subsubsection{Evaluation Metrics}
Task-level metrics are accuracy \& F1-score \cite{vanrijsbergen1979} (SST-2, QQP) and Pearson \cite{pearson1895} / Spearman correlation \cite{spearman1904} (STS-B) \cite{wang2018glue}. System-level federated metrics include communication cost per round, convergence speed, and total training time.

\subsubsection{Implementation Details}
All experiments use learning rate $2\times10^{-5}$ with 1,000-step warmup, batch size 8 (centralized) or 8--16 (federated), weight decay 0.01, gradient clipping (max-norm 1.0), and random seed 42. Centralized runs train for 25 epochs; federated clients perform 1 local epoch per round. Communication uses ports 8771--8775 with 60\,s client timeout and 3,400\,s round timeout, plus a 3-retry exponential-backoff mechanism.

Figure~\ref{fig:perf_trajectory} plots model accuracy / correlation as experimental complexity increases from Centralized Single-Task through Centralized MTL, Federated Single-Task, Federated MTL (Non-IID), Federated MTL (IID), and Federated MTL with LoRA fine-tuning.

  \caption{%
    Performance Trajectory.

    across six paradigm-complexity stages for all five SLM architectures.
    Dashed vertical lines separate centralized from federated settings.

    communication-efficient fine-tuning despite maximal system complexity.}
  \label{fig:perf_trajectory}
\end{figure*}

Key observations from Figure~\ref{fig:perf_trajectory}:
\begin{itemize}
  \item Reliability gap: classification accuracy (SST-2, QQP) degrades moderately (${\le}5\%$) in federated settings (Table~\ref{tab:results_consolidated}), while STS-B Pearson correlation shows larger drops - up to 64 points for TinyBERT under Non-IID MTL.
  \item MTL benefit: comparing FL-Single and FL-Multi (Non-IID) reveals task-interaction effects (Table~\ref{tab:results_consolidated}); SST-2 suffers modest negative transfer whereas QQP can benefit from shared representations.
  \item LoRA recovery: LoRA \cite{hu2021lora} reduces communication overhead while partially recovering performance relative to the Non-IID MTL baseline, especially for larger models (DistilBERT, BERT-Medium).
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Discussion}
% ------------------------------------------------------

\subsubsection{Trade-offs Between Privacy, Performance, and Efficiency}
The results in Figures~\ref{fig:perf_trajectory}--\ref{fig:time_trajectory} reveal fundamental trade-offs. Federated learning preserves data privacy and reduces per-client resource usage by 74--93\% (Table~\ref{tab:results_consolidated}) but increases total training time by 2.2--3.7$\times$ compared to centralized training. Small language models enable edge deployment at the cost of bounded task performance for complex NLU applications such as semantic similarity.

\subsubsection{Insights into Task Transfer and Negative Transfer}
Classification tasks (SST-2, QQP) demonstrate better positive transfer characteristics than regression (STS-B), as seen in Table~\ref{tab:results_consolidated}. Negative transfer is most pronounced in STS-B under Non-IID MTL, with Pearson correlation dropping by up to 64 points for TinyBERT (from 0.6474 to 0.0081). Larger models (DistilBERT, BERT-Medium) are more resilient to negative transfer due to higher representational capacity. Notably, DistilBERT under Federated MTL (IID) achieves 0.9461 accuracy on SST-2 (Table~\ref{tab:results_consolidated}), outperforming its centralized MTL counterpart.

\subsubsection{Practical Deployment Considerations}
TinyBERT is the best option for extremely resource-constrained devices ($<0.1$\,GB per client), but with notable performance penalties on STS-B. DistilBERT (highlighted in red in Table~\ref{tab:results_consolidated}) offers the best performance--efficiency balance for most federated multi-task scenarios, maintaining over 0.92 accuracy on SST-2 even under Non-IID conditions. LoRA \cite{hu2021lora} is recommended for bandwidth-limited deployments as it reduces training time by 38--62\% for most models while maintaining competitive accuracy.
