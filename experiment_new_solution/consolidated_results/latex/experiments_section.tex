\section{EXPERIMENTS}
\label{sec:experiments}

\subsection{Experimental Setup}

\subsubsection{Datasets and Tasks}
The evaluation employs three core NLU benchmarks representing diverse natural language understanding challenges from the GLUE benchmark \cite{wang2018glue}.

SST-2 \cite{socher2013sst} is a binary classification task for sentiment polarity detection, comprising 66,477 training, 872 validation, and 1,821 test samples. Performance is measured by accuracy and F1-score \cite{vanrijsbergen1979}.

QQP \cite{sharma2019qqp} is a binary classification task for paraphrase detection, with 323,415 training, 40,431 validation, and 39,081 test samples. Evaluation uses accuracy and F1-score \cite{vanrijsbergen1979}.

STS-B \cite{cer2017stsb} is a regression task for semantic similarity scoring over sentence pairs on a 1–5 scale, with 4,249 training, 1,500 validation, and 1,379 test samples. Performance is measured by Pearson \cite{pearson1895} and Spearman correlation \cite{spearman1904}.

\subsubsection{Federated Data Partitioning}

IID Partitioning distributes data uniformly across clients (Experiments 5-6 in Table~\ref{tab:design_matrix}, 3 clients, multi-task scenarios).

Non-IID Partitioning uses a Dirichlet distribution ($\alpha{=}0.5$) to simulate realistic data heterogeneity, with 3 clients per task (9 total), yielding 107,805 / 22,159 / 1,416 samples per client for QQP, SST-2, and STS-B respectively (Experiments 7-10 in Table~\ref{tab:design_matrix}).

Validation sets are \emph{not} split across clients in any paradigm, ensuring consistent global model assessment.

\subsubsection{Baselines}
Three baselines are evaluated: (1) Centralized MTL: all 5 SLM variants trained on centralized data with shared encoder and task-specific heads, serving as the upper-bound reference (Exp.\ 1 in Table~\ref{tab:design_matrix}); (2) Federated Single-Task: per-task federated experiments with 3 clients and non-IID data (Exps.\ 8-10 in Table~\ref{tab:design_matrix}); and (3) Large-model federated approaches: comparison against larger LMs to assess communication efficiency and performance trade-offs.

\subsubsection{Evaluation Metrics}
Task-level metrics are accuracy \& F1-score \cite{vanrijsbergen1979} (SST-2, QQP) and Pearson \cite{pearson1895} / Spearman correlation \cite{spearman1904} (STS-B) \cite{wang2018glue}. System-level federated metrics include communication cost per round, convergence speed, and total training time.

\subsubsection{Implementation Details}
All experiments use learning rate $2\times10^{-5}$ with 1,000-step warmup, batch size 8 (centralized) or 8-16 (federated), weight decay 0.01, gradient clipping (max-norm 1.0), and random seed 42. Centralized runs train for 25 epochs; federated clients perform 1 local epoch per round. Communication uses ports 8771-8775 with 60\,s client timeout and 3,400\,s round timeout, plus a 3-retry exponential-backoff mechanism.

\subsubsection{Overall Performance (Comparison across Baselines)}
The comprehensive results in Table~\ref{tab:results_consolidated} demonstrate that Small Language Models (SLMs) integrated within a federated multi-task framework maintain high NLU performance across diverse tasks. Centralized MTL serves as the performance upper bound, yet DistilBERT under Federated MTL (IID) surprisingly achieves 0.9461 accuracy on SST-2, slightly outperforming its centralized counterpart. MiniLM also exhibits robust performance, reaching 0.8966 accuracy on QQP in the Federated MTL setting. These results validate that despite the inherent constraints of FL, SLMs can achieve competitive results relative to idealized centralized training.

\subsubsection{Effect of Multi-Task Learning}
MTL provides significant advantages in federated settings by leveraging shared representations across related tasks. As shown in Figure~\ref{fig:perf_trajectory} and Table~\ref{tab:results_consolidated}, comparing FL-Single and FL-MTL (Non-IID) reveals complex task interactions. While SST-2 suffers modest negative transfer in certain configurations, regression tasks like STS-B often suffer more pronounced drops under Non-IID conditions. However, classification tasks generally demonstrate better positive transfer; for instance, QQP benefits from the features learned via SST-2. Larger SLMs like DistilBERT and BERT-Medium show higher resilience to negative transfer due to their greater representational capacity.

\subsubsection{Federated Learning Performance}
The transition from centralized to federated learning introduces a "Reliability Gap," characterized by a moderate degradation (${\le}5\%$) in classification accuracy for tasks like SST-2 and QQP. This gap is most visible in the STS-B Pearson correlation, which drops significantly for the smallest architectures like TinyBERT under Non-IID MTL conditions. Figure~\ref{fig:perf_trajectory} maps this trajectory, showing how the proposed Federated MTL framework (highlighted in red for IID and dark yellow for Non-IID for the DistilBERT model in Table~\ref{tab:results_consolidated}) manages to close this gap by stabilizing performance across tasks.

Quantitatively, the red metrics (IID) represent the framework's optimal performance, showing that MTL can yield better results than isolated centralized training for specific models (e.g., DistilBERT reaching 0.9461 on SST-2). The dark yellow metrics (Non-IID) highlight the framework's resilience under data heterogeneity; while classification tasks like SST-2 maintain stable performance (e.g., 0.9232 for DistilBERT), regression tasks like STS-B exhibit greater sensitivity, dropping from 0.8258 to 0.4219 in BERT-Medium. This demonstrates that while the framework is remarkably robust for categorical benchmarks, regression stability is heavily dependent on model capacity and data distribution.

\subsubsection{Real-Time Inference Analysis}
For deployment on edge devices where real-time inference is critical, resource consumption and model footprint are the defining metrics. Figure~\ref{fig:resource_trajectory} and Table~\ref{tab:resource_trajectory} highlight that federated learning reduces individual client GPU memory demands by 77-93\% relative to centralized training. TinyBERT and BERT-Mini emerge as the leading candidates for extremely resource-constrained devices ($<0.1$\,GB and $<0.2$\,GB per client respectively), albeit with bounded performance on complex tasks. MiniLM and DistilBERT provide a superior performance-efficiency balance for most real-time scenarios, maintaining high accuracy while keeping per-client resource usage significantly lower than the centralized baseline.

\subsubsection{Communication Efficiency}
Communication overhead is the primary bottleneck in federated MTL, with wall-clock training times increasing by 2.2-3.7$\times$ compared to centralized training (Table~\ref{tab:time_trajectory}). However, as illustrated in Figure~\ref{fig:time_trajectory}, the integration of parameter-efficient fine-tuning via LoRA \cite{hu2021lora} substantially mitigates this cost. LoRA reduces federated training time by 38-62\% for most models by transmitting only low-rank adapter weights instead of full model parameters. This optimization enables the framework to maintain competitive accuracy while drastically reducing the bandwidth requirements, making it suitable for bandwidth-limited edge deployments.
