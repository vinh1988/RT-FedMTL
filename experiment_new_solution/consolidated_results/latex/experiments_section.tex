\section{EXPERIMENTS}

\subsection{Experimental Setup}

\subsubsection{Datasets and Tasks}
The evaluation employs three core NLU benchmarks representing diverse natural language understanding challenges.

\textbf{SST-2} is a binary classification task for sentiment polarity detection, comprising 66,477 training, 872 validation, and 1,821 test samples. Performance is measured by accuracy and F1-score.

\textbf{QQP} is a binary classification task for paraphrase detection, with 323,415 training, 40,431 validation, and 39,081 test samples. Evaluation uses accuracy and F1-score.

\textbf{STS-B} is a regression task for semantic similarity scoring over sentence pairs on a 1–5 scale, with 4,249 training, 1,500 validation, and 1,379 test samples. Performance is measured by Pearson and Spearman correlation.

\subsubsection{Federated Data Partitioning}

\textbf{IID Partitioning} distributes data uniformly across clients (Experiments 5--6, 3 clients, multi-task scenarios).

\textbf{Non-IID Partitioning} uses a Dirichlet distribution ($\alpha{=}0.5$) to simulate realistic data heterogeneity, with 3 clients per task (9 total), yielding 107,805 / 22,159 / 1,416 samples per client for QQP, SST-2, and STS-B respectively (Experiments 7--10).

Validation sets are \emph{not} split across clients in any paradigm, ensuring consistent global model assessment.

\subsubsection{Baselines}
Three baselines are evaluated: (1) \textbf{Centralized MTL} --- all 5 SLM variants trained on centralized data with shared encoder and task-specific heads, serving as the upper-bound reference (Exp.\ 1); (2) \textbf{Federated Single-Task} --- per-task federated experiments with 3 clients and non-IID data (Exps.\ 8--10); and (3) \textbf{Large-model federated approaches} --- comparison against larger LMs to assess communication efficiency and performance trade-offs.

\subsubsection{Evaluation Metrics}
Task-level metrics are accuracy \& F1 (SST-2, QQP) and Pearson / Spearman correlation (STS-B). System-level federated metrics include communication cost per round, convergence speed, and total training time.

\subsubsection{Implementation Details}
All experiments use learning rate $2\times10^{-5}$ with 1,000-step warmup, batch size 8 (centralized) or 8--16 (federated), weight decay 0.01, gradient clipping (max-norm 1.0), and random seed 42. Centralized runs train for 25 epochs; federated clients perform 1 local epoch per round. Communication uses ports 8771--8775 with 60\,s client timeout and 3,400\,s round timeout, plus a 3-retry exponential-backoff mechanism.

Key observations from Figure~\ref{fig:perf_trajectory} and Table~\ref{tab:perf_trajectory}:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Reliability gap}: classification accuracy (SST-2, QQP) degrades moderately (${\le}5\%$) in federated settings, while STS-B Pearson correlation shows larger drops — up to 72 points for TinyBERT under Non-IID MTL.
  \item \textbf{MTL benefit}: comparing FL-Single and FL-Multi (Non-IID) reveals task-interaction effects; SST-2 suffers modest negative transfer whereas QQP can benefit from shared representations.
  \item \textbf{LoRA recovery}: LoRA reduces communication overhead while partially recovering performance relative to the Non-IID MTL baseline, especially for larger models (DistilBERT, BERT-Medium).
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Discussion}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------

\subsubsection{Trade-offs Between Privacy, Performance, and Efficiency}
The results in Figures~\ref{fig:perf_trajectory}--\ref{fig:time_trajectory} reveal fundamental trade-offs. Federated learning preserves data privacy and reduces per-client resource usage by 77--93\% but increases total training time by 2.4--3.9$\times$ compared to centralized training. Small language models enable edge deployment at the cost of bounded task performance for complex NLU applications such as semantic similarity.

\subsubsection{Insights into Task Transfer and Negative Transfer}
Classification tasks (SST-2, QQP) demonstrate better positive transfer characteristics than regression (STS-B). Negative transfer is most pronounced in STS-B under Non-IID MTL, with Pearson correlation dropping by up to 72 points for TinyBERT. Larger models (DistilBERT, BERT-Medium) are more resilient to negative transfer due to higher representational capacity.

\subsubsection{Practical Deployment Considerations}
TinyBERT is the best option for extremely resource-constrained devices ($<0.1$\,GB per client with LoRA), but with notable performance penalties on STS-B. DistilBERT offers the best performance--efficiency balance. LoRA is recommended for bandwidth-limited deployments as it reduces both communication volume and training time while maintaining competitive accuracy on classification tasks.
