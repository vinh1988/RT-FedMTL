\section{INTRODUCTION}
\label{sec:introduction}

% \subsection{Background and Motivation}
With the proliferation of NLU systems on mobile devices, enterprise platforms, and edge services, the demand for privacy-preserving collaborative learning has grown at an alarming pace. In many practical scenarios, NLU data includes sensitive user information (e.g., personal messages, medical text, behavioral logs) making it challenging to collect centralized data due to privacy and regulatory constraints. FL resolves such shortcomings by storing raw data on client devices and only sharing model updates \cite{mcmahan2017fedavg}. At the same time, distributed AI systems are expected to support many users, domains, and tasks in parallel \cite{mcmahan2017fedavg,smith2017mocha}. The trend makes centralized single-pipeline training less feasible and encourages federated training models, which can learn collaboratively without breaking the data locality.

% \subsection{Challenges in Federated Natural Language Understanding}
While FL is a promising technique for NLU, there are three major concerns. First, client data usually is non-IID. Given that diverse users and organizations write language data with different vocabularies, label compositions and preferences, convergence is typically slowed, and global model quality is poor \cite{li2020fedprox,smith2017mocha}. Second, task heterogeneity is common in realistic deployments. A federation could be made with intent classification, sentiment analysis, question answering, or even generation-related goals all at once \cite{smith2017mocha,chen2024fedbone,zhou2025mfed}. Classical one-model FL methods are not suited to this mixed-task regime. Third, communication and scalability are essential. In general, the repeated syncing of multiple clients can lead to significant overhead, particularly when the size of the model is large and the connected clients unstable \cite{mcmahan2017fedavg,chen2024fedbone}. Recent literature on federated multi-task learning (e.g., MOCHA, FedBone, MIRA, encoder--decoder based M-Fed) indicates that data heterogeneity and task heterogeneity need to be considered as first-class design considerations \cite{smith2017mocha,chen2024fedbone,elbakary2025mira,zhou2025mfed}.

% \subsection{The role of small language models in federated systems}
Small Language Models (SLMs) are especially appealing in federated systems because they improve efficiency without losing language pretraining functionality. Key advantages include:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Communication efficiency: A smaller parameter set reduces the communication cost per round (both upload and download).
    \item Reduced computation cost: SLMs use fewer resources of local memory and training time, allowing for broader client involvement.
    \item Edge-device deployment: compact models are easier to deploy and update on resource-constrained devices.
\end{itemize}
This role translates into parameter-efficient adaptation techniques (e.g., adapter-style and low-rank updates) that preserve competitive performance while decreasing federated overhead.

% \subsection{Real-time federated learning for NLU}
The vast majority of existing FL pipelines are batch-based: clients train on multiple local steps and sync periodically \cite{mcmahan2017fedavg,li2020fedprox}. This design is strong on static datasets, but it is not as well suited to streaming and dynamic NLU, in which patterns of language use, subjects, themes and users' intent evolve over time. Real-time federated learning seeks to reduce feedback loops by aiding in speeding up synchronization, ad-hoc adaptation, and more timely model updates. In online NLU applications (such as chatbots, live moderation, event-based analytics, etc.), latency of global updates can degrade performance very quickly. Thus, the implementation of real-time synchronization mechanisms seems to be on the rise for relevance and robustness of production systems.

% \subsection{Contributions of This Work}
Expanding on this motivation, and the reviewed literature, this work proposes:
\begin{itemize}
    \item A real-time FL-MTL approach for heterogeneous NLU tasks.
    \item Small language models in federated settings to increase efficiency and deployability.
    \item Analysis of how data heterogeneity affects FL-MTL convergence and downstream task performance.
    \item Evaluation across different NLU benchmark tasks, local and global.
\end{itemize}
Herein, the rest of the paper is structured as follows. Section~\ref{sec:related_work} reviews the relevant work in federated and multi-task learning. Section~\ref{sec:problem_definition} defines the formal problem of RT-FMTL. In Section~\ref{sec:proposed_method} we detail the system architecture and optimization strategies. The experimental setup and results analysis are described in Section~\ref{sec:experiments}. Last but not least, Section~\ref{sec:conclusion} wraps up the paper and presents the next steps.
