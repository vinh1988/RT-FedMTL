\section{CONCLUSION}
\label{sec:conclusion}

In this work, we presented a comprehensive investigation into real-time federated multi-task learning using Small Language Models across diverse Natural Language Understanding benchmarks. Our systematic evaluation across five architectures provided critical insights for the deployment of decentralized NLU systems. Our results demonstrate that federated learning effectively preserves data privacy while significantly optimizing resource distribution, achieving up to 93\% reduction in per-client GPU resource consumption. However, the transition to real-time federated multi-task environments introduces notable synchronization overheads, as observed in our study using WebSocket-based communication.

A key challenge identified in our work is the impact of task heterogeneity. We found substantial negative transfer effects in regression tasks (STS-B) under Non-IID distributions, particularly for the smallest model variants (TinyBERT and BERT-Mini). Heterogeneity-aware aggregation and Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) proved essential for communication efficiency, though LoRA involves a trade-off in performance stability for complex tasks. Among the investigated SLM architectures, DistilBERT consistently offered the most robust performance, showing superior resilience to the negative transfer effects inherent in multi-task federated learning. For scenarios where memory is the primary bottleneck, architectures like MiniLM provide a viable alternative, though with less stability in regression regimes.

Future research will focus on developing adaptive task-sharing strategies to further mitigate negative transfer in multi-head architectures. Additionally, we plan to explore personalized federated learning techniques and the integration of differentially private mechanisms within the real-time communication layer, ensuring that efficiency gains do not compromise privacy in production NLU deployments.
